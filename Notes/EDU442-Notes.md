### 6.1 2026-01-26
**Topic:** Overview of the course
**Bell, D. (2018). _Faces at the Bottom of the Well_ (Introduction, pp. 1-14).**
- **Core claim:** racism is not an occasional deviation; it is a durable, self-reproducing feature of U.S. life and institutions.
- **Move:** uses narrative + legal/political analysis to argue that “progress” often masks new forms of the same racial order.
- **What to watch for:** how he explains *why* many white people resist reforms, and how poverty/segregation are framed as produced by policy (not “culture”).
- **EQUITAS lens:** treat “diagnosis” and “assessment” as political technologies that can reproduce structural racism even when they look objective.

### 6.2 2026-02-02
**Topic:** Beginning the journey
**hooks, b. (1994). Teaching to Transgress - Ch. 3: “Embracing Change: Teaching in a Multicultural World” (pp. 35-44).**
- **Core claim:** multicultural education requires paradigm shifts (teachers + institutions), not token inclusion at the margins.
- **Key ideas:** education is never politically neutral; “difference” creates tension; students need compassion while learning critical lenses.
- **Practical strategy:** build classroom community by ensuring every student is heard (voice, journaling, structured sharing), and explicitly teach about the discomfort of shifting paradigms.
- **EQUITAS lens:** a “neutral” model is still political; your evaluation choices (labels, features, outcomes) embed assumptions unless interrogated.

**Talusan, L. (2022). _The Identity-Conscious Educator_ (Introduction + Part 1).**
- **Core idea (quick orientation):** inclusive practice is built from repeatable habits (not just values): noticing identity, interrogating power, and acting with accountability.
- **Skim target:** definitions of “identity-conscious” practice, and any habit/checklist frameworks you can operationalize in observation notes and future PD modules.
- **EQUITAS lens:** translate identity-conscious habits into “audit questions” for datasets, metrics, and deployment contexts.

### 6.3 2026-02-09
**Topic:** Intersectionalities (Group A posts)
**Leo, A. (2023). “Toward a class-conscious approach to cultural responsiveness.”**
- **Core claim:** cultural responsiveness often becomes shallow if it ignores class and material conditions; class-consciousness changes what “responsive” even means.
- **What it adds:** a corrective to “culture-only” approaches by making economic power and structural constraints visible in pedagogy and policy.
- **EQUITAS lens:** fairness can’t be evaluated without class-informed context (access, time, money, instability) shaping the data and the stakes.

**Schalk, S., & Kim, J. B. (2020). “Integrating race, transforming feminist disability studies.”**
- **Core claim (study orientation):** disability frameworks change when race is not an “add-on” but co-constitutive; disability and race mutually shape lived experience and theory.
- **EQUITAS lens:** do not treat disability and race as separable variables; intersectional design changes both the questions you ask and what “harm” looks like.

**Kimberlé Crenshaw TEDTalk: “The Urgency of Intersectionality.”**
- **Core claim:** intersectionality names how power systems collide; single-axis stories erase people who sit at multiple intersections.
- **EQUITAS lens:** justify intersectional evaluation (e.g., subgroup audits beyond single categories) and explain why “overall accuracy” hides patterned harm.

### 6.4 2026-02-16
**Topic:** Critically raced (Group B posts) + Self-Portrait due
**Coles, J. A. (2023). “A BlackCrit re/imagining of urban schooling social education through black youth enactments of Black storywork.”**
- **Core claim (study orientation):** Black youth storytelling and “storywork” can function as theory and praxis - not just “data” to extract.
- **EQUITAS lens:** design research methods that treat participants as knowledge producers; avoid extractive “model training data” logics.

**Ladson-Billings, G., & Tate, W. (1995). “Toward a critical race theory of education.”**
- **Core claim:** racism is structural, and schooling reproduces it; CRT provides tools to analyze policy, curriculum, and inequality.
- **EQUITAS lens:** frame “diagnostic inequity” as produced by structures (policy, resource allocation, categories), not only individual bias.

**Pizarro, M., & Kohli, R. (2020). “I Stopped Sleeping”: Teachers of Color and the Impact of Racial Battle Fatigue.**
- **Core claim (study orientation):** repeated racial stress in schools produces cumulative exhaustion and health impacts for teachers of color.
- **EQUITAS lens:** “implementation context” includes adult well-being; tools/policies that increase surveillance or conflict intensify harm for educators and students.

### 6.5 2026-02-23
**Topic:** Rethinking pedagogies
**Blackburn, M. V. (2021). “Pedagogy and pleasure: Trans and gender transgressive students in an LGBTQ-themed literature class.”**
- **Core claim:** “pleasure” can be pedagogically and politically meaningful - not extra - for trans/gender-transgressive students navigating school.
- **Move:** examines how classroom practices can create space for desire/joy/agency (and not only protection-from-harm frameworks).
- **EQUITAS lens:** include outcomes that capture belonging and agency; “harm reduction only” is insufficient as a definition of equity.

**Paris, D., & Alim, H. (2014). “What are we seeking to sustain through culturally sustaining pedagogy? A loving critique forward.”**
- **Core claim (study orientation):** culturally sustaining pedagogy argues for sustaining community cultural/language practices, not “including” them while assimilating students.
- **EQUITAS lens:** define “success” as sustaining and expanding students’ cultural-linguistic repertoires, not optimizing for standardization/assimilation.

### 6.6 2026-03-02
**Topic:** Intersectionality, identity, and decolonization (Group A posts)
**Gill, V. (2022). “Intersectional(ity) pedagogy: Conceptualizing soul work toward solidarity and resistance.”**
- **Core claim:** intersectional pedagogy requires “soul work” - analysis of self and society aimed at solidarity, healing, humanization, and resistance.
- **Setting:** yearlong inquiry as a 6th grade English teacher (charter school serving marginalized/first-gen students).
- **EQUITAS lens:** make “reflexivity” part of method: how your positionality and institutional incentives shape what you call evidence and success.

**Werito, V. (2025). “What You Know You Hold Onto It ... that is Being Navajo”: Diné youth perspectives about race, culture, language, identity.**
- **Core claim:** Diné/Navajo youth construct racial, cultural, and linguistic identities within settler-colonial and racialized U.S. contexts.
- **Lens:** uses Tribal Critical Race Theory (TribalCrit) to foreground Indigenous sovereignty and colonial histories.
- **EQUITAS lens:** identity categories are political; any “diagnostic” system must account for historical context and avoid collapsing Indigenous identity into generic race categories.

### 6.7 2026-03-09
**Topic:** Linguistic diversity (Group B posts)
**Austin, T., & Anya, U. (2024). “World languages for Black Linguistic Reparations.”**
- **Core claim:** world language education in the U.S. has historically erased Black learners and Black linguistic histories; the field can be a site of reparative work.
- **Proposal:** reparations via redistributing resources, repairing teaching practices, and re-narrating “the world” beyond a white Western lens.
- **EQUITAS lens:** language-related assessment must treat linguistic difference as expertise and history, not error to be corrected.

**Baker-Bell, A. (2020). “Dismantling anti-black linguistic racism in ELA classrooms: Toward an anti-racist Black Language pedagogy.”**
- **Core claim:** “academic language” and classroom norms often enact anti-Black linguistic racism; teachers can dismantle it via anti-racist Black Language pedagogy.
- **Move:** historicizes debates, names “anti-black linguistic racism,” and offers a classroom-based example leading to instructional implications.
- **EQUITAS lens:** audit “standard language ideology” in rubrics, labels, and NLP/assessment tools; do not equate whiteness with correctness.

**Optional:** Smitherman, G. (1999). Intro to ebonics.
- **Use:** background to support a historical and linguistic framing of Black Language(s) as rule-governed, meaningful, and culturally embedded.

### 6.8 2026-03-16
**Topic:** School-to-prison pipeline (Group A posts) + 13th note-catcher due
**Watch:** _13th_ (Ava DuVernay, 2016).
- **Core arc:** traces connections between slavery, the 13th Amendment’s punishment clause, criminalization, mass incarceration, and political economy.
- **EQUITAS lens:** “risk scores” and disciplinary tools become pipeline infrastructure when deployed in unequal systems.

**James-Gallaway, C. D. (2022). “The Kids in Prison Program”: CRT personal counternarrative (KIPP / No Excuses).**
- **Core claim:** No Excuses discipline and surveillance practices dehumanize Black students; the paper documents racialized discipline and “fugitive” acts of resistance.
- **Context:** post-Katrina New Orleans reforms; whitened educator workforce; charter CMO power.
- **EQUITAS lens:** surveillance and behavior-tech can be “fair” on paper and still reproduce domination through school governance and policy logics.

**Marsh, V. (2017). “Becoming restorative: Three schools transitioning to a restorative practices culture.”**
- **Core idea (orientation):** restorative shifts require culture change and sustained investment; they fail when treated as a quick behavior program.
- **EQUITAS lens:** implementation matters more than slogans; measure whether practice changes relationships and power, not just referrals.

**Stevenson, B. (2021). “Punishment” (in _The 1619 Project_, pp. 431-456).**
- **Core idea (orientation):** punishment is historically racialized; carceral logics shape law, schooling, and everyday life.
- **EQUITAS lens:** “discipline data” is never neutral; it is produced inside a punishment regime.

### 6.9 2026-03-23
**Topic:** Class, schooling, and precarious knowing (Group B posts)
**Desmond, M. (2021). “Capitalism” (in _The 1619 Project_, pp. 259-306).**
- **Core idea (orientation):** capitalism in the U.S. is entangled with racial exploitation; inequality is produced, not accidental.
- **EQUITAS lens:** resource deprivation changes what schools can do and what students can access; models must be evaluated under constraints.

**Fine, M., Greene, C., & Sanchez, S. (2016). “Neoliberal blues and prec(ar)ious knowledge.”**
- **Core claim (orientation):** neoliberal reforms produce precarious conditions that reshape what counts as knowledge and whose knowing is valued.
- **EQUITAS lens:** if your system assumes stable resources/time, it will fail (and blame students) in contexts shaped by austerity.

**Talusan, L. (2022). Ch. 4 (Class).**
- **Skim target:** practice habits for noticing class-based exclusion and changing routines/policies that quietly sort students.

### 6.10 2026-03-30
**Topic:** Gender, gender identity, and sexuality
**Blair, E., & Deckman, S. (2022). “Beyond pronouns: The case for gender-expansive and democratizing practice in teacher education.”**
- **Core claim:** moving past “pronouns-only” inclusion toward broader democratizing practices in teacher education.
- **EQUITAS lens:** inclusion can’t be reduced to one variable or one ritual; design for structural participation and safety.

**Rende Mendoza, K., & Johnson, C. (2024). “A (TRANS)formative approach to gender-inclusive science education.”**
- **Core claim:** argues for gender-inclusive science education as transformative work; offers a framework for practice/teacher learning.
- **EQUITAS lens:** science “objectivity” has a gendered history; inclusion requires changing norms of participation and what is treated as legitimate knowledge.

**Talusan, L. (2022). Ch. 6 (Gender).**
- **Skim target:** classroom habit shifts and case examples you can port into your Impacting Practice artifact.

### 6.11 2026-04-06
**Topic:** The “F” word (Group A posts)
**Bianca, H. (2020). “Extending bell hooks’ feminist theory.”**
- **Core idea (orientation):** revisits hooks’ feminist theorizing to extend/clarify implications for contemporary pedagogy and praxis.
- **EQUITAS lens:** resist universalism; ask whose experience is centered when you define the “default user/student.”

**Grande, S. (2003). “Whitestream feminism and the colonialist project.”**
- **Core idea (orientation):** critiques feminism that reproduces settler/colonial logics; calls for decolonizing commitments.
- **EQUITAS lens:** avoid “equity” frameworks that still center dominant epistemologies as the baseline.

**Roberts, K. M. (2021). “Integrating feminist theory, pedagogy, and praxis into teacher education.”**
- **Core claim:** proposes ways to integrate feminist theory with teaching practice in teacher education; emphasizes praxis (reflection + action).
- **EQUITAS lens:** make your work legible as praxis: explicit theory -> method -> practice -> accountability.

### 6.12 2026-04-13
**Topic:** Inclusion and constructing others (Group B posts) + Deconstructing Bias due
**Annamma, S., Connor, D., & Ferri, B. (2013). “Dis/ability critical race studies (DisCrit).”**
- **Core claim (study orientation):** DisCrit theorizes race and disability as intertwined; critiques how schools label, sort, and punish.
- **EQUITAS lens:** disability classification systems are racialized; design/evaluate tools as part of this history, not outside it.

**Flynn, S. (2024). “Critical disability studies and the affirmative non-tragedy model: presenting a theoretical frame for disability and child protection.”**
- **Core claim:** proposes an affirmative, non-tragedy framing of disability within child protection contexts, grounded in critical disability studies.
- **EQUITAS lens:** avoid deficit/tragedy narratives in diagnostics; build systems that treat disability as a valued form of human variation.

**Talusan, L. (2022). Ch. 7 (Disability).**
- **Skim target:** language guidance + practical routines to avoid ableist assumptions in school decision-making.

### 6.13 2026-04-20
**Topic:** Joy and refusal (Impacting Practice presentations / milestones)
**Edwards, E. B., & Reynolds, A. D. (2025). “You Can’t Steal My Joy! Practices and Possibilities for Black Joy in Urban Education.”**
- **Core claim:** positions Black Joy as an analytic and praxis orientation in urban education, linked to refusal, love, spirituality, and eroticism.
- **Use:** treat joy as methodological and pedagogical (not just affect), and as a guidepost for research and practice.
- **EQUITAS lens:** incorporate justice-linked flourishing outcomes; avoid “risk-only” frameworks.

**Okello, W. K. (2025). “Unspeakable Joy: Anti-Black Constraint, Loopholes of Retreat, and the Practice of Black Joy.”**
- **Core claim:** theorizes Black Joy under constraint; joy is practiced through retreat/loopholes within anti-Black conditions.
- **EQUITAS lens:** equity work must account for constraint; evaluate interventions by whether they expand the space to breathe/live, not only reduce negatives.

**Vlach, S. K., Lentz, T. S., & Muhammad, G. E. (2023). “Activating Joy Through Culturally and Historically Responsive Read-Alouds.”**
- **Core claim:** joy is a rigorous pursuit; gives educators CHRE’s five pursuits (identity, skills, intellect, criticality, joy) with concrete read-aloud examples centered on justice and humanity.
- **EQUITAS lens:** treat joy as part of the learning objective; build evaluation that captures identity, criticality, and joy (not only skills).

### 6.14 2026-04-27
**Topic:** Liberatory futures (roundtable)
**Toliver, S. R. (2022). “Dreamland: Black Girls Saying and Creating Space through Fantasy Worlds.”**
- **Core claim:** analyzes fantasy stories written by Black middle school girls to show how they resist spatial control and create worlds they can construct/dismantle.
- **EQUITAS lens:** futurity and imagination are educational outcomes; “reimagining futures” is not fluff - it is resistance.

**Naraian, S. (2021). “Making inclusion matter: critical disability studies and teacher education.”**
- **Core claim:** uses critical disability studies (including posthumanist threads) to challenge teacher-ed tenets (school critique, agency/change, narrative experience) and argues for an “affirmative critique.”
- **EQUITAS lens:** inclusion work is not “outside” the system; it requires confronting ontological entanglement of schools/teachers/scholars (accountability, not distance).

### 7.2 Social capital and Bourdieu - “shifting codes of the elite” (video)
1 - Watch the video  
The video explains how elites maintain social boundaries through **cultural codes** (taste, language, credentials, norms). When the middle class adopts a marker that once signaled “elite,” elites often **shift the code** (move to a new marker) so distinction still works. In Bourdieu terms: elites protect their position in a “field” by converting and displaying **cultural capital** (what counts as refined/legitimate), **social capital** (networks and gatekeepers), and **symbolic capital** (recognition/status). One key performance is “**caring not to care**” - showing ease, effortlessness, and “natural” belonging, which hides the resources behind success.

2 - How have you seen this phenomenon play out in your own life.  
I’ve seen it in academic and professional spaces where some people can present achievement as effortless. The “care-not-to-care” stance shows up as: acting casual about grades, opportunities, or connections while quietly having support systems (time, money, mentorship, safety nets) that make those outcomes possible. I’ve also experienced the pressure to learn unwritten rules quickly (how to speak in meetings, what “counts” as impressive, how to network) - and noticing that once a code becomes common (a resume format, a club, a credential), the “top” version shifts (more exclusive programs, more niche experiences, more insider language).

3 - Howe have you seen this phenomenon play out in schools  
In schools, it shows up in how students with more resources build advantage through **social capital** (parents’ networks, access to tutors, insider information) and then treat the resulting success as normal. For example, wealthy students can:
- get help fast (private tutoring, editing, test prep, paid programs) and still perform “effortlessness”
- access relationships that function like shortcuts (recommendations, introductions, “someone who knows someone”)
- accumulate credentials that look merit-based but are also resource-based (summer programs, travel, research “experience”)
It also shows up in classroom norms: confidence and “professional” speech get rewarded as competence, even when that’s really **cultural capital** (habitus) shaped by family and schooling.

4 - How have you seen this phenomenon play out in the world  
In the wider world, you can see elites shifting status signals as soon as something becomes widely accessible. For example:
- moving from obvious luxury (logos) to “quiet luxury” (subtle, hard-to-recognize markers)
- shifting from *what you own* to *what you know* (restaurants, art, travel, “taste,” insider references)
- turning access itself into a marker (private spaces, invitation-only events, exclusive networks)
Even spending that seems “unreasonable” can be doing symbolic work: it communicates belonging, security, and access. The key point is that the code is not fixed - it changes to keep the boundary in place.
