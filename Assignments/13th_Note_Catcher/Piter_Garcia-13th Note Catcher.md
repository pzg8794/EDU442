What ideas, assumptions, or emotional responses did you bring into the film?

|  |
| :---- |
| Before watching, I already understood mass incarceration as racially disproportionate, but I think I still carried a fairly segmented view of U.S. history: slavery, Jim Crow, civil rights, and prisons as related but somewhat separate chapters. Emotionally, I expected anger and grief, but I also expected a more conventional documentary about prison statistics rather than a sweeping argument about the continuity of racial domination through law. I also came in thinking about schools, because once punishment becomes a default social logic, it is difficult not to see its smaller-scale versions in classrooms, discipline systems, and so-called behavioral interventions. |

 What felt familiar *before* the documentary began making its argument?

 

|  |
| :---- |
| What felt familiar was the broad claim that Black communities are overpoliced and overpunished, and that political rhetoric about “crime” is rarely neutral. The connection between fear, media messaging, and policy also felt familiar. What was less familiar was the film’s insistence that these are not merely modern failures of reform but extensions of a constitutional and historical structure that never fully severed punishment from racialized labor and social control. |

In your opinion, what is *13th* ultimately arguing about race, crime, punishment, and the state?  
 Where does the argument feel most clear? Where does it feel more complicated or unsettled?

|  |
| :---- |
| I think *13th* is ultimately arguing that race, crime, and punishment in the United States cannot be understood separately. The film argues that the state has repeatedly remade systems of racial control by attaching Blackness to criminality and then using law, policing, prisons, and political language to make that linkage appear necessary or natural. The argument feels most clear when the documentary traces a line from slavery to convict leasing to Jim Crow to mass incarceration and shows that each system preserved domination through a new legal vocabulary. Where it feels more complicated is in the breadth of the coalition the film critiques: because it implicates both parties, media institutions, corporate actors, and public sentiment, it raises the harder question of what structural change would actually be sufficient, rather than what reform would simply rename the same system. |

 The film repeatedly returns to the amendment’s exception clause.

How does DuVernay frame this clause historically and politically?  
 What does it permit or normalize?  
 What tensions does it raise for you between legality and justice? 

How do these influence your future/work in the classroom?

|  |
| :---- |
| DuVernay frames the exception clause as a hinge between formal emancipation and legalized punishment. Historically, the clause allowed unfreedom to be reconstituted through criminal conviction, which made Black criminalization politically useful and economically profitable. The film suggests that the clause does not just sit passively in the Constitution; it helps normalize the idea that coercion, confinement, and forced labor can become legitimate once a person has been marked “criminal” by the state. That raises a serious tension between legality and justice: something can be lawful and still reproduce domination. For my future work in the classroom, that tension matters because schools also rely on legalistic and bureaucratic categories that can feel objective while still doing harm. A rule, referral process, behavior rubric, or attendance policy can be formally neutral and still function as a mechanism of sorting, exclusion, and punishment. The film pushes me to ask not only whether a school policy is authorized, but whose humanity it protects, whose behavior it pathologizes, and whether it expands care or expands surveillance. |

 

Which historical moments felt most clearly connected to the present?  
 Where did you notice repetition, recycling, or rebranding of racial control?

|  |
| :---- |
| The clearest historical repetitions were the movement from slavery into convict leasing, from Jim Crow into “law and order” politics, and from explicitly racist control into race-evasive language about crime, safety, and personal responsibility. What struck me most was not just repetition, but rebranding: the language changes while the underlying social function remains recognizable. Instead of openly defending white supremacy, public discourse shifts toward “public safety,” “superpredators,” “drug wars,” or “tough on crime” frameworks that still disproportionately target Black communities. That same logic feels present today in predictive tools, surveillance technologies, zero-tolerance policies, and risk-based sorting systems that claim neutrality while reproducing unequal outcomes. |

## **Power, Language, and Narrative**

Pay attention to *how* the story is told.

How are terms like *crime*, *law and order*, *safety*, or *criminal* constructed?  
 Who gets to define these terms in the film—and who does not?

 

|  |
| :---- |
| In the film, terms like *crime*, *law and order*, *safety*, and *criminal* are not treated as neutral descriptions. They are shown as political language shaped by those with institutional power: politicians, police, media organizations, and the broader state. “Law and order” is constructed as a moral and common-sense response to danger, even when it functions as cover for racialized punishment. “Safety” is framed as something promised to the public, but the film asks whose safety is being imagined and whose suffering is made acceptable in the name of that promise. The category *criminal* becomes especially powerful because once a person or population is named that way, extraordinary violence and dispossession can be justified as deserved. The people most affected by these labels rarely get to define them; instead, they are spoken about, governed, and managed by institutions that claim authority over them. |
